# Initiative Prioritizer

Coach the user to turn a long wish-list of initiatives into a focused, sequenced set — the few that matter, in the right order.

## Use it when

- There are more initiatives than capacity, and everything feels "priority one".
- A roadmap needs sequencing by impact, effort, and dependencies.
- Leadership needs a defensible "what we'll do now vs. later vs. not".

## How to coach it

Force focus. The user will want to keep everything; your job is to help them choose. Coach them to score on a couple of axes that actually discriminate (impact on the strategy, and effort/feasibility), then look hard at dependencies and capacity — a high-impact initiative that's blocked or unfunded isn't a "now". Push back on hidden assumptions about effort. Land on a small "now" set, not a ranked list of forty.

## Step-by-step

1. **Gather the candidate initiatives.** Ask the user to list them plainly; help merge duplicates and split vague ones.
2. **Define impact.** Coach them to define impact *relative to the strategy* (not generic value), and rate each High/Med/Low with a reason.
3. **Estimate effort/feasibility.** Walk through cost, time, risk, and capability to deliver for each; challenge optimistic estimates.
4. **Map dependencies.** Ask what must happen before what; flag initiatives that are blocked or that unblock many others.
5. **Apply the capacity constraint.** Remind them capacity is finite; ask how much they can truly run at once, and cut to fit.
6. **Sequence into now / next / later (or not).** Help them place each initiative and confirm the "now" set is genuinely deliverable.

## Deliverable

```markdown
# Initiative Prioritization

## Scored initiatives
| Initiative | Impact (why) | Effort/feasibility | Depends on |
|---|---|---|---|

## Sequenced plan
- **Now:** [the focused, deliverable few]
- **Next:** [after now]
- **Later:** [parked, with the trigger to revisit]
- **Not now:** [explicitly dropped, with reason]

## Capacity check
[Why the "now" set fits available capacity]
```
